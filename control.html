Control Systems Reference Guide
System Block Diagram

This tool models a standard feedback loop. A cascade compensator C(s) sits in the forward path with the plant G(s). A feedback compensator H(s) modifies the feedback signal. Gain K scales the entire forward path.

Root Locus Fundamentals

The root locus plots how the closed-loop poles move as K varies 0→∞. Poles in the right half-plane (Re > 0) cause instability. The locus starts at open-loop poles and migrates toward open-loop zeros or infinity.

Construction rules:
• Number of branches = OL pole count
• Branches start (K=0) at OL poles ×
• Branches end (K→∞) at OL zeros ○ or ∞
• Always symmetric about real axis
• Real segments: odd number of OL poles+zeros to the right

Stability legend:
× = open-loop poles
○ = open-loop zeros
◆ = closed-loop poles at current K

STABLE: all CL poles in LHP
MARGINAL: poles on jω-axis
UNSTABLE: pole(s) in RHP

Compensator Types — Equations & Usage
P — Proportional
C(s) = Kp
Multiplies error by a constant. Simple but cannot eliminate steady-state error in type-0 plants. High gain improves speed but reduces stability margins.
▶ WHEN: plant already stable, need fast action
I — Integral
C(s) = Ki / s
Adds a pole at origin. Guarantees zero steady-state error for step inputs by accumulating error over time. Reduces phase margin significantly.
▶ WHEN: must eliminate SS error; plant needs type upgrade
PI — Proportional-Integral
C(s) = Kp(s + z) / s
Integrator with a zero at s=−z to recover some phase lost by the pole. Eliminates SS error while softening the stability penalty of pure I action.
▶ WHEN: need zero SS error with acceptable damping
PD — Proportional-Derivative
C(s) = Kp(s + z)
Adds a zero, increasing phase margin and transient speed. Amplifies high-frequency noise — in practice always paired with a roll-off pole: (s+z)/(s+p), p≫z.
▶ WHEN: sluggish plant, need faster settling, low noise environment
PID — Full PID
C(s) = K(s² + as + b) / s
Two zeros plus integrator. Most common industrial controller. Zeros cancel or offset plant poles. Eliminates SS error, improves transient, but requires tuning.
▶ WHEN: general-purpose; unknown plant dynamics
Lead Compensator
C(s) = K(s + z)/(s + p), z < p
Zero is left of (closer to origin than) the pole. Contributes positive phase near crossover, increasing phase margin and bandwidth. Speeds up the response.
▶ WHEN: need more phase margin and faster response
Lag Compensator
C(s) = K(s + z)/(s + p), z > p
Pole is left of zero. Provides high DC gain (improving SS error) while barely affecting high-frequency response. Slightly reduces bandwidth and phase margin.
▶ WHEN: need better SS accuracy without changing transient much
Notch Filter
C(s) = (s²+ω₀²) / (s²+2ζω₀s+ω₀²)
Places complex zeros at a mechanical resonance frequency to cancel it. Used to suppress lightly-damped structural modes that would cause instability at high gain.
▶ WHEN: plant has a resonance peak causing instability
Tachometer (Feedback)
H(s) = Kt · s / (s + p)
Feeds back velocity (derivative of position). Equivalent to inner derivative feedback: adds damping without amplifying input noise. Used in motor/servo drives.
▶ WHEN: motor control; need derivative damping without forward-path D
How to Enter Transfer Functions

Coefficient format:
Enter space-separated coefficients, highest power first.

s³ + 3s² + 2s + 1
→ 1 3 2 1

s(s+1)(s+2)
→ 1 3 2 0

1/(s²+0.5s+4)
Num: 1 Den: 1 0.5 4

Design workflow:
1. Choose a plant G(s) (try a preset)
2. Enable C(s), select PID or Lead
3. Sweep K slider — watch locus
4. Pick K where ◆ diamonds sit in LHP with ~45° angle from negative real axis (good damping)
5. Switch to step response, check overshoot <20%, SS error near zero
6. Tune compensator zeros to reshape locus
